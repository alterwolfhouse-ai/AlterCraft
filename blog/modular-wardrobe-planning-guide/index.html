Modular wardrobe design guide for Indian bedrooms.

A good modular wardrobe is not just a shutter finish. It is a measured storage system that fits the room, the bed clearance, Indian clothing, daily routines, luggage and the way the family actually uses the bedroom.

Many wardrobe projects fail because planning starts from a photo. The shutters look nice, but the inside does not match the owner. Shelves become messy, drawers hit the bed, hanging height is wrong, suitcases have no place and the loft becomes hard to use. The better method is simple: measure the room, list what you own, then design the wardrobe around access and daily movement.

Quick answer

For most Indian bedrooms, a practical modular wardrobe needs clear front movement, around 600 mm depth for hanging clothes, a mix of short hanging and shelves, drawers around waist or lower-mid height, loft storage for low-use items and shutter type chosen by bed clearance.

Measure the room before choosing the design

Start with the wall where the wardrobe will sit. Note the wall width, ceiling height, skirting, beams, columns, switchboards, window positions, AC drain lines, door swing and the bed location. Then measure the walking space in front of the wardrobe.

This front clearance decides more than people expect. If openable shutters hit the bed or block the passage, the wardrobe will annoy the user every day. If the room has comfortable clearance, openable shutters may give better access and easier servicing.

Standard wardrobe dimensions to use as a starting point

Final dimensions should come from site measurement, but these planning ranges help you understand what the designer or carpenter is proposing. Treat them as practical guidelines, not as a fixed promise for every room.

Wardrobe part Practical planning range Why it matters
Depth for hanging Around 600 mm Gives shirts, jackets and hangers enough space without pressing the shutter.
Compact shelf depth 450 to 550 mm where needed Can work for shelves, bags or utility storage when room depth is tight.
Openable shutter width Usually kept comfortable for hinge load Very wide shutters feel heavy and can need stronger hinges.
Drawer zone Waist to lower-mid height Drawers are easiest to see and use when they are not too high or too low.
Loft storage Top zone above daily reach Best for suitcases, blankets, bedding and seasonal items.

Sliding vs openable wardrobe doors

The door decision should come from room movement, not trend. Sliding shutters save front clearance because they move sideways. Openable shutters need swing space, but they allow full access to the internal section and are simpler to service.

Choice Best for Tradeoff
Sliding wardrobe Compact bedrooms, bed close to wardrobe, modern clean front Usually one side opens at a time, track quality matters, cleaning is needed.
Openable wardrobe Rooms with front clearance, full internal access, simpler service Needs shutter swing space and careful hinge choice for heavier shutters.
Mirror shutter Rooms needing dressing support or visual openness Placement matters because glare and reflections can feel uncomfortable.

Plan internal layout from your clothing inventory

The inside of the wardrobe should be planned like a map of what you own. A household with mostly folded daily wear needs a different layout from a household with sarees, long dresses, suits, bags, blankets and luggage.

Daily wear

Keep it in the centre

Daily clothes should sit where the hand naturally reaches, not in the loft.

Indian wear

Plan long hanging

Sarees, lehengas, sherwanis, long dresses and coats need longer hanging zones.

Small items

Use drawers

Accessories, innerwear, belts, watches and documents become messy on open shelves.

Low-use items

Use the loft

Suitcases, blankets, extra bedding and seasonal pieces belong in top storage.

Simple zoning rule
  • Eye and hand level: daily-use clothes, folded stacks and quick-access items.
  • Lower-mid level: drawers, heavier folded clothes and accessories.
  • Side bay: short and long hanging zones.
  • Top loft: suitcases, bedding, blankets and occasional items.

Materials, finishes, hardware and accessories

Material and finish affect price, durability, maintenance and the way the bedroom feels. Laminate is practical for many everyday wardrobes. Acrylic gives a glossier look but needs more care. Veneer can feel warmer and richer, but it needs better handling and finishing discipline.

Hardware matters just as much as finish. Hinges, drawer channels, handles, sliding tracks, locks and soft-close fittings decide how the wardrobe behaves after daily use. Do not spend everything on the visible shutter and then compromise the movement system.

Lighting and ventilation are easier before fabrication

Sensor lights, profile lights and dressing lights should be discussed before wardrobe fabrication. After production, adding clean wiring can become difficult. If the room is humid or the wardrobe sits near an exterior wall, also discuss ventilation gaps and breathable planning.

Wardrobe lighting is especially useful in deep shelves, dark corners and dressing zones. It can also become short-video content because customers understand the value quickly when they see one dark wardrobe and one planned wardrobe side by side.

Cost planning: what changes the final quote

Modular wardrobe cost is not decided only by length. The quote changes with height, depth, board type, shutter style, finish, hardware, drawer count, accessories, loft storage, lighting, transport and installation scope.

Listed ready furniture on the AlterCraft site has visible product pricing. Custom modular wardrobe projects are different: they are quoted after measurement and scope review. As a planning direction, AlterCraft modular wardrobe projects usually start around Rs. 85,000 and can go higher with size, finish, sliding systems, drawers, lighting and premium hardware.

Quote tip

When asking for a quote, send wall width, ceiling height, room photos, bed position, shutter preference and a rough list of what the wardrobe must store. That makes the first estimate far more useful.

Common wardrobe planning mistakes to avoid

  1. Choosing the shutter finish before measuring the room.
  2. Using only shelves and then wondering why the wardrobe becomes messy.
  3. Ignoring bed clearance and shutter swing.
  4. Keeping drawers too high or too low for daily use.
  5. Forgetting a luggage, bedding or blanket zone.
  6. Not planning lighting and switches before fabrication.
  7. Copying a showroom layout without listing your own clothing inventory.

Modular wardrobe FAQ

What is the best way to plan a modular wardrobe?

Start with measurements, front clearance, clothing inventory and daily-use zones. Then decide shutter type, internals, materials, hardware, lighting and finish.

What is the standard depth for a wardrobe?

Around 600 mm is a practical depth for hanging clothes. Smaller depths can work for compact shelves, but hanging rods need enough depth for hangers and clothes.

Is sliding or openable wardrobe better?

Sliding works better when front clearance is tight. Openable works better when there is enough space and the user wants full access to each internal section.

How should wardrobe internals be divided?

Use a mix of hanging space, shelves, drawers, daily-use storage and loft storage. The exact split should come from your clothing inventory.

What changes modular wardrobe cost?

Size, material, shutter style, finish, drawers, accessories, lighting, hardware, loft storage and installation scope all affect the quote.
